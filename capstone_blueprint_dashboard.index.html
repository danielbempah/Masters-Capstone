The three most common structural failures in scholar-practitioner capstones — with specific fixes tailored to your document and context.

Pitfall 1
Describing frameworks instead of deploying them

The most common failure is devoting large amounts of space to explaining what Kolb's cycle is, what andragogy means, or what the political economy of technology argues — and then never using those frameworks to make an original analytical move. The document becomes a textbook summary rather than a piece of scholarly-practitioner reasoning. Readers who know these frameworks are bored; readers who don't are left wondering why they matter.

How to avoid it in your document

Adopt a strict rule: every framework gets no more than one short paragraph of definition, followed immediately by a sentence that begins "In the context of immigrant-owned MSMEs in the EAC program, this means..." If you cannot write that sentence, you are not ready to include the framework. Apply this test in every chapter.

Pitfall 2
The program review that stays descriptive

When scholar-practitioners review comparable programs — Google's B(AI)sics, SBA initiatives, community college digital equity programs — they frequently describe what each program does without making a comparative argument. The result is a list of programs with no analytical payoff. Your analysis of the Google/Chamber program is one of the most intellectually sharp moments in your current draft — you identify the platform dependency problem and the equity gap — but that critical lens needs to be applied consistently across all program examples, not just one.

How to avoid it in your document

Build a simple evaluative matrix — three to four criteria drawn directly from your research objectives (equity by design, tool-agnosticism, scalability, community embeddedness) — and run every program example through it. This transforms description into analysis and makes the gap your project fills visible and defensible. The matrix can appear as a figure in the document.

Pitfall 3
Recommendations that are too abstract to act on

Capstone recommendations chapters frequently end with calls to action that are simultaneously obvious and impossible to operationalize: "policymakers should invest in digital equity," "future researchers should examine long-term outcomes," "programs should be culturally responsive." These statements are true but useless. They signal that the author ran out of analytical energy in the final chapter, which is precisely where funders and senior practitioners are most closely reading.

How to avoid it in your document

For each recommendation, answer three questions: Who specifically should do this (ESD, SBA, city EDC, EAC coordinators)? What specifically should they do differently from what they do now? What would success look like in 18 months? If you cannot answer all three, the recommendation is not ready to write. Aim for five to seven recommendations with this level of specificity rather than ten that are vague.
